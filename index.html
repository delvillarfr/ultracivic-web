A climate action so simple, you can DIY.

Coal, oil, and gas-fired power plants in the Northeast need a state-issued allowance to emit a ton of CO2. They can trade their allowances before they use them, and so can you! To stop a ton of carbon from exiting their chimneys, buy an allowance, and lock it up.

Three steps to DIY:

Proof.

This is the story: power plants are planning to use every available allowance to pollute. When you buy a pollution allowance and lock it up, power plants have one fewer allowance, so they cut their emissions by one ton.

Our story is true if four things are true: that power plants' emissions are well-measured, that states in the Northeast do not issue more allowances because you bought one and locked it up, that power plants never pollute without an allowance, and that power plants will use every available allowance to pollute. Let's examine each of these ;)

A. Power plants' emissions are measured well.

The Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) monitors the hourly carbon emissions of coal, oil, and gas-fired power plants and publishes the data under the Clean Air Markets Program. It requires power plants to install devices that continuously measure the carbon dioxide moving up inside their chimneys, or install fuel flow measuring systems to monitor how much they burn. Coal power plants must install the devices in their chimneys.

To dig deep, explore the Clean Air Markets Program Data here , and look at the monitoring and reporting section of the regulations of states in the Northeast: Connecticut (p. 28), Delaware (p. 34), Maine , Maryland , Massachusetts (p.466), New Jersey (p.79), New Hampshire (p.41), New York , Rhode Island , and Vermont (p.50). They build on the federal emissions reporting requirements listed in Part 75 of the Code of Federal Regulations.

B. There is a predetermined number of allowances available to power plants.

Back in 2017, the Northeastern states decided how many new pollution allowances to issue every year from 2020 to 2030. They have issued allowances according to plan, so they do not issue new ones on a whim, e.g., if they see that you bought one and locked it away.

Feeling mole-y? Look at the planned allowance issuances in the section on allowance allocations in each of the states' regulations: Connecticut (p.16), Delaware (p. 19), Maine , Maryland , Massachusetts (p.455), New Jersey (p. 51), New Hampshire (p. 29), New York , Rhode Island , and Vermont (p. 32). Compare them with the actual issuances, available here .

C. Power plants do not pollute without allowances.

Every couple of years, power plants have to surrender to the states an amount of allowances equal to the tons of carbon dioxide that they emitted. If they do not, states make them buy three allowances for every ton that they did not account for. That is bad for power plants, so they buy enough allowances to cover their emissions.

To see that power plants actually surrender the right number of allowances, check out the data here : pick a source (i.e., power plant), pick a control period, get the report, and compare the total emissions with the total allowances deducted.

D. Power plants use every available allowance to pollute.

This one is an economics twister :) Power plants pay top dollar for an allowance, between $20 and $26 nowadays. Allowances are valuable to them only because they let them pollute. If they were not going to use them for that purpose, they would be crazy to pay anything for them! Since they are not crazy, they must plan to use every available allowance.
