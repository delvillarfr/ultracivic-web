Proof.

Northeastern states know their power plants' emissions very well, and decided how many permits to issue many years ago. Time to dive into each of the claims we just made :)

A. Power plants' emissions are measured well.

The Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) monitors the hourly carbon emissions of coal, oil, and gas-fired power plants. It requires them to install devices that continuously measure the carbon dioxide moving up inside their chimneys, or install fuel flow measuring systems to monitor how much they burn. Coal power plants must install the devices in their chimneys.

To dig deep, look at the monitoring and reporting section of the regulations of states in the Northeast: Connecticut (p. 28), Delaware (p. 34), Maine , Maryland , Massachusetts (p.466), New Jersey (p.79), New Hampshire (p.41), New York , Rhode Island , and Vermont (p.50). They build on the federal emissions reporting requirements listed in Part 75 of the Code of Federal Regulations.

B. There is a predetermined number of permits available to power plants.

Back in 2017, the states decided how many new pollution permits to sell to power plants every year from 2020 to 2030. They have issued permits according to plan, so they do not issue new ones on a whim, e.g., if they see that you bought one and locked it away.

Feeling mole-y? Look at the planned permit issuances in the section on allowance allocations in each of the states' regulations: Connecticut (p.16), Delaware (p. 19), Maine , Maryland , Massachusetts (p.455), New Jersey (p. 51), New Hampshire (p. 29), New York , Rhode Island , and Vermont (p. 32). Compare them with the actual issuances, available here .

C. Power plants do not pollute without permits.

Every couple of years, power plants have to surrender to the states an amount of permits equal to the tons of carbon dioxide that they emitted. If they do not, states make them buy three permits for every ton that they did not account for. That is bad for power plants, so they buy enough permits to cover their emissions.

To see that power plants actually surrender the right number of permits, check out the data here : pick a source (i.e., power plant), pick a control period, get the report, and compare the total emissions with the total allowances deducted.

D. Power plants use every available permit to pollute.

This one is an economics twister :) Power plants pay top dollar for a permit, between $20 and $26 nowadays. Permits are valuable to them only because they let them pollute. If they were not going to use them for that purpose, they would be crazy to pay anything for them! Since they are not crazy, they must plan to use every available permit.
